The degree to which a person, community or infrastructure system is susceptible to future climate hazards.

PREAMBLE AND COMMITMENTS
This strategic document will determine how Istanbul will achieve its carbon neutral target by 2050. 
S ince the Paris Agreement was reached in 
December 2015, climate change has been a central topic on global and national political agendas. The Paris Agreement calls for the global temperature increase to be kept below 2°C, preferably to a maximum of 1.5°C, compared to pre-industrial levels and for every nation in the world to play its part.
At the same time as countries are working to reduce emissions, they are also taking action to strengthen their resilience to the effects of climate change, particularly where these impacts affect vulnerable populations.
Although climate change is a problem that is global in nature, action can be taken locally and by all stakeholders, including by individuals. 
Today, 55% of the world's population lives in cities and this is expected to rise to 68% by 
2050. Due to their high population, resource use, and infrastructure requirements, cities emit significant greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions – and these populations and systems are also impacted by the effects of climate change, be that rising summer temperatures, floods, or drought. 
Cities around the world are showing leadership on climate action, not least the group of cities that are members of the C40 Cities Climate 
Leadership Group. Cities in this cohort have committed to Deadline 2020, a programme that commits cities to delivering a “fair share” pathway to zero carbon by 2050. 
As a member of the C40 Cities Climate 
Leadership Group, the Istanbul Metropolitan 
Municipality (IMM) signed the Deadline 2020 document in October 2019 and adopted its goal of becoming a carbon neutral and climate resilient city by 2050. This increased level of commitment means the city needs to develop and adopt a revised climate action plan – a strategic document setting out how Istanbul will achieve its 2050 carbon neutral goal. 
This report has been prepared by the IMM team within the context of the C40 Technical 
Assistance Program specially offered by C40 for Istanbul.

LETTER FROM MAYOR ear Istanbulites,
Our world, which has existed for billions of years and welcomed us, has long signalled that we are at an extremely critical threshold. At this critical threshold, everyone has a lot of responsibilities. We are working hard to make Istanbul a strong actor of solidarity in taking action against climate change with our belief that this struggle will result in a collective victory.
The measures to be taken on climate change, one of the greatest threats facing our world, are our last chance to show our true loyalty to our world. As we have experienced once again with severe weather events events, storms, floods, droughts, temperature rises and especially the 
COVID-19 pandemic, nature's responses have shown that they have no limits. We are not free from these reactions, but we recognize that we are also responsible for this unique planet. By 
2050, cities are expected to be home to about 
68% of the human population. In metropolises, which are one of the most critical actors in this process, we need rational and holistic policies, accelerating climate action and ensuring climate justice.
Programmes such as the Sustainable 
Development Goals, the Global Green New 
Deal, and the European Green Agreement, which define targets, indicators, and methods in the field of climate change on a global scale, focus on management. As the Istanbul 
Metropolitan Municipality (IMM), we are aware of the urgency in localizing these goals and the role of cities in combating climate change. 
From here, we consider our “Climate Change 
Action Plan” to be an important initiative in the fight against the climate crisis.
At IMM, we follow a sustainable and holistic method by integrating the fight against climate change into different policies. Signing the Deadline 2020 Commitment at the C40 
Mayors Summit in Copenhagen in 2019, we have adopted Istanbul's goal of becoming a “carbon neutral” and “resilient city” by 2050. Following this, we have started the revision studies of our current Climate Change Action Plan to be in line with Deadline 2020 Commitments and Paris Agreement goals. We analysed how the current plans and policies of Istanbul will affect the increase in greenhouse gasses by 
2050. To meet the 2050 commitments, we have determined new policies and strategies that can be developed for emission reduction.
We are happy to be able to take steps towards our climate policies despite the difficult pandemic conditions in Istanbul, well as all over the world. Our Green and Fair Recovery Plan, which we have prepared in this process, is our main roadmap in our fight against COVID-19.
With the vision of “Fair, green, and creative 
Istanbul”, we have taken important steps such as developing a sustainable and environmentally friendly transportation strategy, increasing the amount of active green space, increasing the share of renewable energy in total energy consumption to 18% by 2024, and increasing the capacity to obtain electricity from waste in 2020 in our city, where more waste is produced than many European countries due to population density. 
Alongside this, we have focused on studies that increase urban resilience in the face of other disasters, such as the risk of major earthquakes, floods, epidemics, and droughts facing our city. 
We have refurbished our rainwater tunnels and reduced flooding with our infrastructure works at many points. 
In order to bring the agricultural areas in 
Istanbul back into production and to support farmers who make a living from agriculture, we contribute to recycling and energy efficiency activities by providing the energy of the seedling production center from waste, as well as supporting our farmers. We continue to work in a multi-faceted way for the circular economy and the green economic transformation of the workforce. 
While carrying out all these activities, we consider the participation and support of the people of Istanbul. Istanbul extremely important. In particular, we continue our studies on this subject such as awareness, education, etc. We have launched one of the world's limited edition climate museums to raise public awareness about the climate crisis. 
One of the most important goals is to make local governments a strong actor while carrying out climate change studies in the international platforms such as C40 cities and Global 
Covenant of Mayors, of which we are a member.
We will make Istanbul a global model city where waste management practices are developing, renewable energy is widespread, energy efficiency is increasing, the fight against climate change is internalized, the amount of active green space per capita is increasing, and where sustainable urban mobility is central. 
We will follow our climate change mitigation and adaptation efforts with the understanding of participation in the light of our Climate 
Action Plan and continue to implement it with determination.

The city of Istanbul has developed action plans and frameworks to support its emission reduction target and tackle climate change. 
The Ministry of Environment Urbanization and Climate Change is responsible for making regulations regarding climate change in Turkey. 
There are currently three strategic documents that summarize the country’s overall policy on climate change. These are:
1- National Climate Change Strategy (2010-2020)
This document defines Turkey's vision for fighting against climate change:
 “Turkey's national climate change vision is to be a country that has integrated its climate change policies with development policies, expanded energy efficiency, increased the use of clean and renewable energy sources, actively participated in the fight against climate change within the framework of its special conditions and can offer high quality of life and prosperity to all its citizens with low carbon intensity.”
In this context, national targets are set. 
In addition, short, medium, and long-term strategies have been identified in the energy, transportation, industry, waste, land use, agriculture and forestry sectors, climate change adaptation, related technology transfer, financing, and capacity building.
2- National Climate Change Action Plan (2011-2023):
Purposes, targets and actions have been defined for common horizontal issues in water resources management, agriculture sector and food security, ecosystem services, biodiversity and forestry, natural disaster risk management, human health and adaptation to climate change. Responsible institutions, deadlines, outputs and performance indicators have been determined for the actions.
These National Climate Change Strategy and 
Action Plan documents listed above show 
Turkey’s efforts to contribute to the global fight against climate change. In these documents, there are targets determined by taking into account the country's unique capacities and conditions.
Public participation and the decisions taken by the city government are very important in directly reducing emissions for some sectors and in determining the roadmap to be followed by the industry and private sector.
3- Turkey’s National Climate Change Adaptation 
Strategy and Action Plan (2011-2023). 
Targets have been defined for common horizontal issues in water resources management, agriculture sector and food security, ecosystem services, biodiversity and forestry, natural disaster risk management, human health, and adaptation to climate change. 1.D. Green and Just Recovery
This Climate Action Plan builds on the many and varied ways the city has responded to the 
COVID-19 pandemic and sets out the action required to ensure an equitable and sustainable recovery. The presented climate actions focus on creating green jobs, investing in crucial public services, protecting public transportation, supporting essential workers, and recovering public spaces to the people and nature.
In line with the C40 Mayor’s Agenda for a Green and Just Recovery, Istanbul commits to taking action on creating jobs and building an inclusive economy by: 
• Creating new, good green jobs in a fast way.
• Supporting and promoting essential workers.
• Training and developing the skills of employees to enable a just transition to an inclusive economy.
The city also prioritises actions that promote resilience and equity. It will continue to provide fundamental public services for all, knowing that they support a fair society and strong economy, and that they are resilient to future shocks. 
• Providing a safe and resilient post-COVID public transportation system.
• Providing fundamental public services for all, such as clean water, food, health, and affordable, healthy housing.
Action on health and well-being is also crucial. 
The city commits to giving public space back to people and nature, recovering the streets from cars and guaranteeing clean air to ensure liveable, local communities. These commitments are aimed to be carried out as follows:
• Creating ‘15-minute cities’ where all residents of the city are able to meet most of their needs within a short walk or bicycle ride from their homes.
• Giving streets back to people, by permanently reallocating more road space to walking and cycling, investing in city-wide walking, and cycling networks and green infrastructure.
• To help reduce the risks of extreme heat, drought, and floods and improve liveability, physical and mental health; developing solutions with nature by giving priority to “nature-based solutions” such as parks, green roofs, green walls, blue infrastructure, and permeable pavements.

G
HG inventories provide a record and baseline of the emissions released over the period of one year. Inventories provide disaggregated data on emission levels and sources, capturing in detail the activities under different sectors and the many different greenhouse gases that result from them.
The inventory estimates all emissions and reports them in carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e). This is a metric measure used to compare the emissions from various greenhouse gases on the basis of their global-warming potential (GWP), by converting amounts of other gases to the equivalent amount of carbon dioxide with the same global warming potential.
The baseline city-wide GHG inventory covers the stationary energy, transportation, and waste sectors. Istanbul's Greenhouse Gas 
Inventory for 2019 was calculated by the 
IMM team according to the Global Protocol for Community-scale Greenhouse Gas 
Emission Inventories (GPC) ‘BASIC’ Standard and approved by the C40 expert team for compliance with international standards. The 
Istanbul Metropolitan Municipality is annually calculating and monitoring its greenhouse gas inventory.
In the GHG inventory, the total base year (2019) emissions in Istanbul were approximately 50.9 
MtCO2e, equivalent to 3.3 tCO2e per capita.

The ambitious pathway shows a clear commitment by the city to reduce emissions despite its significant growth. As the city overcomes the barriers to further action, they will work towards the Extended Ambition

Nonetheless, it is important to prepare for any remaining emissions that may occur in 2050. 
These are called ‘residual emissions’ and will usually be in hard-to-treat sectors, or otherwise completely outside the city’ control. IMM will keep an up-to-date estimate of residual emissions through the annual emissions inventory reporting and future pathways modelling.

Carbon offsetting will allow us to cancel out emissions that could not be reduced at their source by financing projects that avoid or sequester CO2 emissions. C l i m a t e
Istanbul has a transitional climate between the 
Black Sea and the Mediterranean and is one of the rainiest cities in the Marmara Region. Due to its location, Istanbul has a complex climate that has some of the climatic characteristics of the Mediterranean region while also interacting with the surrounding climatic zones. 
In the Mediterranean climate zone, summers are hot and dry, and winters are warm and rainy. The most important factor in the arid summer season is that the Hadley circulation cell shifts to the north and strengthens the 
Azores high pressure system. Low-pressure systems originating from the Atlantic, which bring precipitation to Turkey in the cold season, cannot penetrate the Mediterranean watershed due to the Azores high that gets stronger in the summer, so precipitation decreases in almost all of the watershed. However, Istanbul is affected by the climates of the surrounding regions as well as the Mediterranean climate. It is open to air currents and rainfalls from Siberia and the Balkans in the north and the Sahara Desert in the south.
The city's lowest temperature throughout the year is -11°C, whilst the highest temperature can reach upwards of 40 °C, and the average relative humidity is 75%. Istanbul is humid all year round with the most humid period of the city between December-January where levels can reach 80-85%.

3.C.ii. Future Climate Hazards
Despite global efforts to reduce GHG emissions, it is anticipated that global temperatures will continue to rise, and the extent to which countries and cities are successful in reducing their use of fossil fuels will determine the level of changes in future climate hazards.
Furthermore, continued uncontrolled urbanization, deforestation and environmental degradation can alter the ability of a city to manage the impacts of these future climate hazards and strengthen its resilience. 
The Mediterranean region is an area of Europe expected to be most affected by climate change. The IPCC's 5th Assessment Report identified three key anticipated impacts that 
Turkey and neighbouring regions are likely to suffer from under increases in global warming:
• Increased river and coastal flooding resulting in an increase in the number of people and economic losses due to increased urbanization, rising sea level, coastal erosion, and peak river flows.
• Greater restrictions on water availability due to increased water demand (for irrigation, power generation, industrial and domestic use), and reduced water flow due to increased evaporation and reduction in seasonal precipitation.
• Increase in the number of people affected by extremely hot weather events leading to negative impacts on health and well-being, losses in labour productivity and income, poorer air quality and increased risk of forest fires.

Increases in average temperatures as a result of global warming will reduce the need for heating provision within winter, although demand will increase in summer due to a greater need for cooling. There also needs to be wider consideration of the changing climate outside of the city, due to the fact that many of the power generation plants are situated beyond the city borders.
Istanbul has been subjected to unplanned urbanization for decades due to the high rate of migration. Unplanned or poorly planned urban settlements have less access to formalized energy infrastructure and other services. 
Despite significant progress in providing gas grid connections to households by IMM, lowincome groups continue to utilize charcoal in the home. Informal infrastructure networks are more vulnerable to climate change hazards as well as secondary impacts such as fires.
Istanbul has a well-developed complex transport system, with the provision of multiple modes of public transport, with extensive walking and cycling and private car use. 3.D. Vulnerable Groups in the 
Population
The level of exposure to climate change is directly related to the development level of societies and inequalities in society. High human development countries like Turkey, often have a more carbon emissions per capita and also have more ecological footprint.34 In that case, climate change will have greater impacts on vulnerable groups such as the elderly, children, women, disabled individuals, immigrants, and refugees, especially on low-income groups. 
Increasing temperatures due to climate change are expected to cause deaths from malnutrition and heat stress. Those with underlying health conditions particularly cardiovascular diseases and respiratory diseases are more vulnerable to the impacts of heat waves, as well as elderly and very young children due to challenges in regulating body temperature. Poor air quality can exacerbate the conditions as aeroallergens are problematic for those with allergic asthma, 
COPD, lung cancer, or other respiratory problems.
Heatwaves and high temperatures can also have an impact on the general population in terms of their health, wellbeing, and work productivity. 
Low-income households and those living in unplanned settlements are more likely to be living in poor quality housing with limited climate change adaptations, including flooding precautions, insulation, cooling measures (air conditioning), green roofs etc. Low-income households also have a reduced capacity to cope and respond to extreme weather events due to reduced financial assets and securities. 
The impact of climatic changes on agriculture and logistics operations will also reduce accessibility to high quality, varied, and nutrient rich foods. This can affect the nutrition of 
Istanbul’s population, particularly the lowincome population who are less likely to have the income to absorb increased food costs as a result of shortages.

3.E. Adaptive Capacity
The adaptive capacity is the capacity of systems and peoples to respond to environmental and societal challenges. Different factors can support or challenge the adaptive capacity, and to varying extents. This in turn can impact resilience. 
The definition by the United Nations of resilience “Is the ability of a system, community or society to withstand, cope with and recover from a disaster, maintaining or regaining its basic structure and functions, quickly and efficiently.” 
Strengthening adaptive capacity through adaptation actions and policies focuses not on eliminating risks, but on reducing the severity of the devastating effects of climate hazards by strengthening the resources and coping mechanisms of systems. These practices include factors such as the improvement of existing infrastructure investments, the planning of new buildings in a more suitable way for the changing climate, and the awareness and preparation of institutions and the public. 
IMM has looked to base its approach to adaptation around three key measures: 
Efficiency, productivity, sustainability.
Governance and institutional infrastructures must be improved and strengthened through activities such as engagement and collaboration. Leadership in this area is crucial for the success of the city’s adaptation approach. As such sufficient budget and human resources should be allocated for adaptation to climate change.
The C40 Climate Change Framework provides a list of factors that may challenge or support the adaptive capacity of a city and its population. 
Using the information included in this report and the broader academic research, the impact of each of these factors has been assessed. 
For example, the high-quality healthcare and almost universal access to primary and secondary education shows that this will be a supporting factor for improving the resilience of Istanbulites – as greater educational attainment has been shown to reduce the vulnerability of populations.39

Mechanism

Timeline
There are several actions that can be deployed immediately, while others will take a longer period to prepare and implement. As actions are developed, key projects will need to be identified and scoped to implement these actions. Details at this level are planned to be added to a longer-term schedule.
Primary impacts
The priority actions will have primary impacts. 
These impacts can be either “direct GHG emissions reduction” or “climate risk reduction”, or a combination of both. 
Barriers
There are a broad range of barriers to the implementation of the priority actions. Across most action areas, ‘political and social’ and ‘financial and economic’ barriers are prominent. 
This highlights the importance of political will to bring forward these priority actions. 
Legal & Institutional
IMM lacks legal authority over several sectors that are key to the implementation of particular strategies or actions. Sectors such as energy efficiency and generation and supply, for example, are centrally controlled at national level. 
IMM is itself unable to implement actions or policy requiring decarbonization of the electricity grid. Similarly, the push towards electric vehicle (EV) uptake is difficult for IMM alone to provide. The regulation for vehicle emissions is also controlled by the national government. IMM does not have control over citizens' choice regarding their private cars, they have only limited influence. 
In buildings, citizens have control over their building heat supply system and building contractors typically install centralized heating systems in large buildings. They tend to choose existing technologies that are tried and tested, and that represent the lowest cost. More efficient technologies, such as heat pumps, are costly when compared to typical systems. 
Though laws or regulations may prevent or limit IMM’s climate action implementation capacities, IMM can and will play an active role in initiating engagement and collaborating with stakeholders to implement projects that extend beyond their areas of authority. 
Financial & Economic
IMM's ability to implement the Climate Change 
Action Plan may be constrained by insufficient financial resources, as well as by some obstacles to implementation. In addition, IMM's access to finance from the private sector and/or climate fund of international financial institutions such as development banks may be limited.
Political & Social
For the Climate Action Plan to be successfully implemented, it will require political and public acceptance and support. IMM will try to implement the determined climate actions, taking into account potential conflicts of authority between central and local authorities.
Practical & Technological
Istanbul’s implementation progress may be constrained by practical limitations, such as the level of development and availability of required technologies to facilitate key actions. 
For example, large-scale renewable energy deployment may require upgrades to the electricity grid to support variable production (i.e., sub-station upgrades). In addition, there is a lack of infrastructure for charging private vehicles. Charging points in the city are not enough.
Overcoming Barriers Through Collaboration
IMM is determined not to allow existing barriers to obstruct progress towards achieving its long-term climate goals and targets. IMM will pave the way for better integration and rollout of climate actions. It will work with other cities across the C40 network to understand how similar barriers have been addressed in other places and seek to adopt innovative approaches to drive change across different stakeholder groups. 
Finance Source 
Most prioritized actions require financial resources that need to be confirmed in detail. 
For Net Zero Buildings, the opportunity to access the municipality's current income sources stands out.

Cities which make up more than 55% of the world's population account for more than 60% of global energy use and more than 70% of global greenhouse gas emissions.
C ities have a unique role and opportunity to shape humanity’s chances of thriving in balance with the rest of the living planet. As home to more than 4 billion people, and over 55% of the world’s population, cities account for over 60% of global energy use, and more than 70% of global greenhouse gas emissions, due to the global footprint of the products they import and consume.40
At the same time, there are vast inequalities in city residents’ experience of urban life, ranging from health, housing, and political representation to access essential services, employment, and wider opportunities. The growing impact of the twenty-first century crises resulting from climate change, the global pandemic and the economic crisis is putting severe and recurrent pressure on many of the world's cities.
As they seek to manage and emerge from these interconnected crises, cities have a critical opportunity to lead in making the transformations needed to create societies and economies that are far more socially just, ecologic, and safe.
With the perspective of getting rid of the devastating effects of COVID-19 among the 
C40 Cities of Istanbul, the Action Plan has been created with the goal of Green and Fair 
Development.

Criteria such as job creation and speed of implementation have directly responded to the economic recovery needed after COVID-19.

IMM and Green Development
Green development is a roadmap for sustainable development as an understanding that prioritizes the investment and consumption of goods and services that contribute to environmental improvements. It argues that all economic activities around the world should be redesigned for environmental purposes.
The budgetary impact on local governments can seem like a huge challenge for cities seeking to become carbon neutral. But green finance needs to be expanded to support economic growth locally, nationally and even internationally. With the benefits obtained from new environmental investments, the financing problems brought on by the economic crisis can be eliminated. 
Istanbul, which constitutes approximately one third of our country's economy, is the economic dynamo of Turkey. Over the past decade, with the ultra-rapid urbanization trend, large waves of internal and external immigration have strained the city's capacity, putting enormous pressure on the environment, and creating distinct challenges for long-term sustainability.
IMM aims an environmentally sensitive and sustainable future for Istanbul with its green development investments. When planning investments, it makes decisions from a point of view that prioritizes environmental impacts. 
In this way, the implementation of green development policies will contribute to the growth of the local economy.

8 - CONCLUSION 
The Mediterranean Region is expectedone of the regions that are expected to be affected the most by climate change in the world. 
T he climate crisis is one of the biggest global problems of our time. Our climate is changing rapidly as a result of human activities such as excessive urbanization, unplanned population growth, seasonal anomalies, and deforestation throughout the world. The Mediterranean region is one of the regions in the world that is expected to be most affected by climate change. Due to its location, Istanbul has a complex climate that both bears the climatic characteristics of the Mediterranean Region and interacts with the surrounding climate zones. For Istanbul, climate risks such as extreme temperature and precipitation change, drought, sea level rise and urban heat island stand out. 
Local governments have an important role as key stakeholders in the fight against the climate crisis. For this reason, IMM signed the Deadline 
2020 commitment accepting the goals of being a carbon neutral and a climate resilient city by 
2050. This commitment becomes reality with the Climate Change Action Plan. This study is a roadmap and strategy document created as a result of comprehensive data collection, modelling, action development and stakeholder engagement stages.
The greenhouse gas emission amount in 2019 is calculated as 50.9 MtCO2e and the per capita carbon footprint is 3.3 tCO2e. Istanbul's 
Greenhouse Gas Inventory for 2019 has been approved by C40 according to GPC Basic level standards. IMM focused on the transportation, waste, building-energy, and water-wastewater sectors, since 63% of emissions belonged to fixed energy, 28% to transportation and 9% to the waste sector. 
Within the scope of action plan studies; 
Business as Usual, BAU; Current and Planned; 
A total of four scenario studies were conducted, namely Assertive and Extended Assertive. 
Within the scenarios, strategies have been determined that will make Istanbul reach its carbon neutral, climate resilient and green development target in 2050. Scoring criteria were chosen by stakeholders and actions were prioritized for each sector. In total, 120 actions were voted according to the scoring criteria and 21 actions were prioritized for the Climate 
Change Action Plan.
Since the prioritized actions include activities that concern many agencies and fall outside the boundaries of municipal authority, cooperation between internal and external stakeholders is needed to achieve success. At this point, IMM has designated climate controllers in order to ensure coordination between its units. 
Within the scope of the planned monitoring methodology, it is aimed to carry out mitigation studies on climate change by meeting with internal climate officials and external sector representatives and to organize consultation meetings on certain routines for adaptation processes. Thanks to the actions and indicators to be monitored annually, the progress made will be monitored annually and the action plan will be revised every five years.
